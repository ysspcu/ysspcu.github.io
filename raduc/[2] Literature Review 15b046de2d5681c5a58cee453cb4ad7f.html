Chapter 1. Literature Review

Overview of Modding Communities

The game modding industry has emerged as a dynamic and transformative force within the gaming ecosystem. Modding, which refers to the practice of altering or adding to video games by users has not only extended the lifespan of many popular games, but has also fostered vibrant communities of creativity and innovation. This literature review examines the development and impact of modding communities, exploring their historical context, the role they play in player engagement and the ongoing research in this field.

Research in this field of game modding has highlighted its multifaceted impact. Studies have shown that modding communities enhance player engagement by providing new content and experiences, which in turn increase the longevity and replayability of games (Schell, 2008). Moreover, the collaborative nature of modding fosters a sense of community among players, encouraging knowledge sharing and collective problem-solving (Hawranke, 2020).

The modding industry has also been a fertile ground for innovation. Many popular game genres and mechanics, such as FPS (First-Person-Shooter) and real-time strategy games have their own origins in user created mods. For instance, the iconic Counter-Strike, originally a mod for Half-Life, eventually became a standalone game that defined the multiplayer shooter genre (Dodge, 2021). Similarly, Defense of the Ancients (DotA), a mod for Warcraft III, laid the foundation for the multiplayer online battle arena (MOBA) genre.

According to Lastowka (2011), the legal status of mods can be ambiguous, leading to disputes between modders and game developers. Furthermore, the quality and safety of mods can vary, with some mods containing malware or causing technical issues. Managing and curating mods to ensure a safe and enjoyable player experience is an ongoing challenge for both developers and the community. Humphreys (2009) emphasizes the need for clear guidelines and support from game developers to help modders navigate these legal and technical challenges.

Intellectual property rights often complicate the relationship between the original creators of the game and the modding community. Developers may welcome mods that enhance the game but may also be wary of those that infringe on their copyrights or introduce unauthorized content.

Ethical responsibilities within modding communities also include maintaining the integrity of the game and respecting the original vision of the developers. Modders must balance their creative freedom with the need to avoid disruptive or harmful changes to the game. The creation of mods that promote negative behaviors, such as cheating or exploiting game mechanics can undermine the gaming experience for others. Community norms and standards often evolve to address these issues, promoting ethical modding practices and fostering a positive environment for all players (Scacchi, 2010).

Moreover, modders have a responsibility to credit the original creators and other contributors appropriately. The collaborative nature of modding means that many projects build upon the work of others, and proper attribution is essential to acknowledge these contributions and maintain trust within the community. As highlighted by Kücklich (2005), transparency and respect for intellectual property are fundamental to sustaining the collaborative spirit of modding communities.

Historical Perspective on Game Development and Mods

Game modding has its roots in the early days of computer gaming, where enthusiasts would modify game code to create new levels, characters and gameplay mechanics. Over the years, modding has evolved from a niche hobby to a significant aspect of the gaming industry. Today, games like Minecraft, The Elder Scrolls V and Grand Theft Auto have thriving modding communities that contribute to their continued popularity and relevance (Lee, Lin, Bezemer, & Hassan, 2020).

Despite its benefits, the modding industry faces several challenges. Legal issues, particularly around intellectual property rights, have been persistent concern. Game developers and modders must navigate a complex landscape of copyright laws and licensing agreements to ensure that mods are legally compliant (Lee et al., 2020). Additionally, the technical complexity of modding can be a barrier to entry for many aspiring developers. The need for specialized knowledge in programming and game design often necessitates extensive learning and collaboration withing the community (Salen & Zimmerman, 2004).

Modding communities have become a significant part of the gaming ecosystem, enabling players to create and share modifications (mods) that enhance or alter gameplay. According to Sihvonen (2011), modding not only extends the lifecycle of a game but also fosters a collaborative environment where players become co-creators. This phenomenon has transformed gaming from a passive experience into an active and participatory one. Modders contribute to the gaming community by introducing new content, fixing bugs, and improving game mechanics. Studies by Nardi and Harris (2006) have shown that modding communities often operate as informal learning environments, where individuals can develop skills in programming, design, and project management. The collaborative nature of these communities promotes knowledge sharing and innovation. Additionally, Scacchi (2010) suggests that modding provides a platform for experimenting with new ideas that can influence mainstream game development.

The advent of user-friendly game development tools and the internet has significantly lowered the barriers to entry for aspiring modders. Kücklich (2005) notes that early modding efforts were often grassroots and amateur, but they have since evolved into sophisticated projects that rival professional game development. Minecraft, in particular, has a rich history of modding. Since its release in 2009, the game's open architecture and supportive community have encouraged extensive modding activities. Notable Minecraft mods such as IndustrialCraft (CurseForge, Industrial Craft), Thaumcraft (CurseForge, Thaumcraft) and BuildCraft (CurseForge, Buildcraft) have introduced new gameplay mechanics, expanded content, and improved performance, highlighting the transformative impact of modding on the game’s evolution (Ollis, B, 2018). Chaplin (2011) discusses how Minecraft's unique approach to modding has created a thriving ecosystem that supports creativity and innovation among its player base.

The Role of Mods in Player Engagement

Mods play a crucial role in enhancing player engagement by providing new content and experiences. Studies have shown that players are more likely to remain invested in a game that offers continuous updates and customization options through mods (Lin & Sun, 2011). Mods cater to diverse player preferences, enabling them to tailor the game to their liking and explore new ways to interact with the game world. Pearce (2011) suggests that the ability to mod a game fosters a sense of ownership and agency among players, which can lead to a more dedicated and active player base. This heightened engagement is not only beneficial for the players but also for the game's longevity and commercial success. In addition, Weststar et al. (2016) argue that mods can create a more inclusive and diverse gaming environment by allowing players to introduce content that reflects their unique cultural and social perspectives.

Furthermore, mods often act as a bridge between developers and the gaming community, facilitating direct feedback and interactive development. According to Postigo (2010), the participatory culture in modding communities empowers players to contribute actively to game development, often leading to the incorporation of popular mods into the official game updates. This collaborative process not only enhances the game, but also strengthens the bond between developers and players.

Modding also contribute to the educational and skill development aspects of gaming. Sihvonen (2011) notes that modding can serve as a gateway to learn programming, graphic design and other technical skills. This hands-on experience is invaluable, as it provides practical knowledge that can be applied beyond gaming, potentially inspiring future careers in technology and creative industries.

The economic impact of mods is another critical aspect worth exploring. Kow and Nardi (2010) highlight that successful mods can drive game sales and sustain long-term interest in a title. According to Postigo (2008), some modders have even turned their hobby into a source of income by monetizing their creations through platforms like Patreon and YouTube. The gaming industry has recognized the value of modding, with companies like Valve and Bethesda integrating mod support and marketplaces directly into their platforms. This symbiotic relationship between developers and modders underscores the economic potential of modding as both a cultural and commercial phenomenon. Parker (2013) highlights how the integration of modding tools in games like Skyrim has contributed to a significant increase in game sales and player retention.

Modding communities often serve as social hubs where players can connect, share knowledge, and collaborate on projects. Pearce and Artemesia (2009) describe how these communities can foster a sense of belonging and collective identity among their members. The collaborative nature of modding projects can lead to the formation of strong social bonds and networks that extend beyond the virtual world. Modding communities also play a crucial role in the development of player-driven narratives and emergent gameplay experiences. Jenkins (2006) discusses how fan-created content can contribute to the cultural and narrative richness of a game, enhancing the overall player experience.

Last but not least modding has been facilitated by the development of robust tools and platforms designed to simplify the process. Minecraft Forge and Fabric Loader are two prominent modding APIs for Minecraft that allow both regular users and developers to create and implement mods easily. These platforms provide a structured framework and a suite of tools that streamline the modding process, making it accessible to a broader range of users (The Ultimate Guide to Minecraft Modding with Java, 2021).

Additionally, MCreator, a comprehensive modding tool, provides an interface and an easy way for users to create simple mods without extensive programming knowledge. MCreator enables users to design and develop mods through a user-friendly graphical interface, allowing for the creation of custom blocks, items, biomes, and more. By lowering the technical barriers, MCreator democratizes the modding process, making it possible for a wider audience to participate in and contribute to the modding community (MCreator: A Comprehensive Guide, 2021).
